Part 1: The 8 templates

Template 1: First-touch (cold)

Subject: quick question Hi [first name], I run an AI services outfit out of Ohio. Spend most of my time helping small business owners under \$5M revenue figure out where AI saves money vs where it's hype. I noticed [SPECIFIC THING ABOUT THEIR BUSINESS — 1 sentence]. Wondering if you've tried [RELEVANT TOOL/PROCESS] yet? If you have, ignore this email. If not, the free 12-tool checklist on our site might be useful: nexusaiconcepts.com/tools-checklist/ Either way, no calls — async only. — Tim

Why each line: "quick question" subject doesn't promise anything (low spam-trigger). First line establishes credibility without bragging. Specific observation proves you read about them. Soft link drop. "No calls" is a contrarian credibility signal.

Link: nexusaiconcepts.com/tools-checklist/ If anything in it raises a question, reply and I'll answer it. — Tim

Template 4: Specific-pain hook

Subject: [their industry] question Hi [first name], Quick one. I notice [their company] is in [INDUSTRY]. The single biggest revenue leak I see in [INDUSTRY] businesses is [SPECIFIC THING — e.g., after-hours leads dying in voicemail]. I wrote a \$14 PDF specifically about that fix. Setup is 5 minutes. Pays back the same week. Link if useful: nexusaiconcepts.com/products/missed-call-playbook/ — Tim

Template 5: The breakup (14+ days, no response)

Subject: closing the loop Hi [first name], I have been reaching out for a couple weeks. Going to take silence as "not the right time" and stop emailing. If something changes, the door's open. If I should never email again, just reply "stop". — Tim

Why this works: Breakup emails get ~3-5x reply rates of standard follow-ups. Loss aversion: people hate the idea of you stopping. Often produces a "actually wait, tell me more" response.

Part 3: Deliverability checklist (run BEFORE sending)

  1. SPF record: verify your domain has an SPF TXT record. Test at mxtoolbox.com/spf.aspx.
  2. DKIM: your sending tool should publish a DKIM key. Verify via mxtoolbox.com/dkim.aspx.
  3. DMARC: as of 2024 Gmail/Yahoo require this for senders >5K/day. Add a TXT record at _dmarc.yourdomain.com with value v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:you@yourdomain.com.
  4. From-name: use a real human name (e.g. "Sam Reilly" or your own name) rather than a generic business name. Personal-sender = Primary tab; business-sender = Promotions tab.
  5. Footer: single-line CAN-SPAM compliant footer. Avoid heavy multi-paragraph footer with hr separator — that's a marketing pattern Gmail filters.
  6. Unsubscribe link: required by law. Make it a real working link.
  7. Image-to-text ratio: if using HTML, keep images under 10% of total content. Image-heavy emails hit Promotions.
  8. Link count: 1-2 links per email. 4+ links = spam signal.
  9. No "spam-trigger" words: avoid "FREE!" (with exclamation), "$$$", "guarantee" (use sparingly), "act now", "limited time". Exception: 1-2 instances of these in long emails is fine.
  10. Plain-text version: if sending HTML, also include a plain-text version. Most ESPs do this automatically.
  11. Test send: send to a Gmail account you control + a Yahoo account + an Outlook account. Check which tab/folder each lands in. Adjust before scaling.

Part 4: Domain warm-up sequence

Fresh sending domains land in spam by default. Warm up over 30 days to build sender reputation:

  • Days 1-3: Send 5-10 emails per day, only to people you know personally who can reply. Reply rate should be 80%+.
  • Days 4-7: Send 15-25 emails per day. Mix of warm contacts and 1-2 prospects who actually engaged with your content.
  • Days 8-14: Send 30-50 per day. Add small batches of cold contacts (10-15 cold per day max).
  • Days 15-21: 50-100 per day. Max 30% cold.
  • Days 22-30: 100-200 per day. Up to 50% cold.
  • Day 30+: Domain is warm. Can scale to 200-300/day cold without spam-folder placement issues.
Skip this and you'll regret it. A fresh domain blasted with 200 cold emails on day 1 will be in Gmail's spam list within 48 hours and stay there for months.

Part 5: Reply-handling scripts

"Stop emailing me" / "unsubscribe"

Got it, removed you from the list. Apologies for the noise. If something changes, the website is at [yoursite] — no signup needed for the free stuff. — Tim

"Tell me more"

Glad it caught your attention. Quick context: [2-3 sentence summary of what you do, written for THEIR specific business] The fastest way to evaluate fit: [link to your strongest landing page]. If you have a specific question, reply with it and I'll answer straight. — Tim

"We're not interested"

All good, appreciate the reply. Closing the loop on my end. If something changes, you know where to find me. — Tim

Auto-reply / out-of-office

Don't respond. The contact didn't actually read your email. Move on.

Part 6: 9 mistakes that kill cold email reply rates

  1. "How are you doing today?" — sounds like a SDR script. Skip the small talk.
  2. "I noticed your company..." followed by something obviously scraped (their LinkedIn job title, their website tagline). Recipients can tell.
  3. Multiple links in the first email. 1 link, max 2. Anything more reads as marketing.
  4. Trying to sound formal. Cold email works best when it reads like a personal email from one person to another. "Hi Tim," not "Dear Mr. Jacobs,".
  5. No specific ask. If you're not asking for something specific, why are you emailing? Either ask for a reply, ask for a click, or ask permission to send something useful.
  6. Subject lines over 40 characters. Mobile clients truncate. Keep it short.
  7. "Just checking in" follow-ups. Vague follow-ups have ~half the reply rate of follow-ups with new value. If you can't add value, send the breakup email instead.
  8. Sending from a brand-new domain to 200+ cold contacts. Spam folder, immediately. See Part 4.
  9. Continuing to email someone who never opens. If your ESP shows 0 opens after 3 emails, drop them. They're either dead-list or filtered to spam.

Copy this Google Sheets template:

  • Recipient email
  • Template used (1-8)
  • Subject line used
  • Opened? (yes/no — pull from your ESP)
  • Replied? (yes/no)
  • Reply sentiment (positive/negative/auto)
  • Followed up? (yes/no/not-yet)
  • Converted? (yes/no)
  • Revenue generated

After 30 days, sort by reply rate per template. Double down on the top performer. Drop the bottom 2.
